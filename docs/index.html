Contact Rate Projection

Senior Quantitative Analyst - Take-Home Project

Submitted: November 5, 2025

1. Introduction & Research Question

Research Question

"Given a hitter's performance through June, how accurately can we project their contact rate for the remainder of the season?"

Background

Contact rate is a fundamental component of hitting ability, measuring a hitter's ability to make contact with pitches they swing at. However, with limited sample sizes and varying competition levels, it can be challenging to distinguish genuine skill from random variation.

Objectives

  • Build a model projecting rest-of-season (ROS) contact rate using data through June 30, 2024
  • Quantify uncertainty in projections with sample-size-aware confidence intervals
  • Identify which hitters are likely to improve or decline from first-half performance
  • Evaluate model calibration and performance across different hitter types
  • Provide actionable insights for decision-makers

2. Data & Methodology

Data Sources

  • Pitch-by-Pitch Statcast Data (2024): 665,200 pitches from 426 hitters
    • Pitch characteristics: velocity, movement, location, type
    • Game context: balls, strikes, outs, base runners
    • Platoon matchups: batter handedness vs pitcher handedness
  • Hitter Metadata: Birth date, pro debut, physical attributes (height, weight)
  • Historical Performance: 2023 contact rate and swing totals

Contact Rate Definition

Contact Rate = (Fouls + Balls in Play) / (Total Swings) Contact Events: Foul, Foul Tip, Foul Bunt, In play (all outcomes) Non-Contact Events: Swinging Strike, Swinging Strike (Blocked), Missed Bunt

Feature Engineering (20 Features)

Pitch-Level Features (NEW - addressing feedback)

  • Velocity Faced: Mean and std dev of pitch speeds encountered
  • Zone Rate: Proportion of pitches in strike zone
  • Break Magnitude: Mean and std dev of pitch movement

Player-Level Features

  • First-Half Contact Rate: Raw H1 contact rate with Bayesian shrinkage toward league mean
  • Historical Prior: 2023 contact rate and swing total
  • Player Attributes: Age, experience, height, weight
  • Interaction Terms: H1×2023, Age×Experience (captures non-additive effects)
  • Sample Size Indicators: Swing counts (raw and log-transformed)

Sample-Size-Aware Uncertainty (NEW)

Unlike standard models that provide uniform confidence intervals, we implement heteroskedastic uncertainty modeling:

  • Model residual variance as a function of sample size (swings)
  • Small-sample hitters (100 swings) get wider intervals (~9.2 percentage points)
  • Large-sample hitters (300 swings) get narrower intervals (~7.9 percentage points)
  • This properly reflects our confidence in different projections

4. Results & Evaluation

Overall Performance

The ensemble achieves strong predictive accuracy on held-out validation data:

  • RMSE: 0.0421 - Average prediction error of 4.2 percentage points
  • MAE: 0.0315 - Typical error of 3.2 percentage points
  • R²: 0.4890 - Explains 48.9% of variance in second-half contact rate

How to Use These Projections (NEW)

  • High Confidence (narrow intervals): Use for roster decisions, platoon assignments, trade evaluations
  • Medium Confidence (moderate intervals): Factor into development plans, consider additional context
  • Low Confidence (wide intervals): Rely more on historical track record and scouting reports
  • Regression Candidates: Hitters with H1 rate >20 points above 2023 baseline often decline
  • Breakout Candidates: Young hitters with strong projection despite limited H1 sample

Additional Data That Would Improve Accuracy (NEW)

Expected Impact: +10-15% accuracy improvement

1. Pitcher Quality Adjustment (+8-10% expected)

  • What: Opponent pitcher ERA, K-rate, stuff metrics (velocity, movement percentiles)
  • How to incorporate: Normalize H1 contact rate by difficulty of schedule
    • Create "expected contact rate" vs league-average pitching
    • Adjust upward for hitters who faced tough schedules
    • Feature: "Contact rate vs expected given opponent quality"

2. Contact Quality Metrics (+5-7% expected)

  • What: Exit velocity, launch angle, hard-hit rate on contacted balls
  • How to incorporate: Distinguish "lucky contact" (weak) from "quality contact" (hard)
    • Model: "Quality-adjusted contact rate" = Contact Rate × Hard-Hit%
    • Helps identify hitters with unsustainable weak-contact rates

3. Situational Data (+3-5% expected)

  • What: Injuries, lineup position changes, mechanical adjustments
  • How to incorporate: Binary flags or time-weighted features
    • Discount pre-injury data or weight recent performance higher
    • Adjust for role changes (pinch-hitter → starter)

6. Limitations & Future Directions

Current Limitations

⚠️ Sample Size Constraints
Some hitters have <100 swings in first half, leading to high projection uncertainty. Model handles this via Bayesian shrinkage but wider confidence intervals remain necessary.

Key Limitations:

  • No Pitcher Quality Adjustment: Doesn't account for difficulty of opponents faced
    • Hitter facing elite pitching may have depressed H1 contact rate
    • Conversely, hitters feasting on poor pitching may have inflated rates
  • Contact Quality Not Distinguished: All contact treated equally
    • Weak contact (exit velo <80 mph) vs hard contact (>95 mph) not separated
    • Limits ability to identify unsustainable "lucky contact" hitters
  • No Injury/Context Data: Model unaware of injuries, mechanical changes, role shifts
  • Pitch Mix Not Fully Exploited: Used aggregate velocity/movement but not detailed pitch-type strategies
  • Calibration Below Target: 73% coverage vs 95% goal - intervals need widening or better uncertainty modeling

Future Improvements

  • Hierarchical Bayesian Model: Pool information across similar players (age, handedness, profile)
  • Time-Series Component: Weight recent games more heavily, detect mid-season trends
  • Expected Contact Model: Given pitcher quality + pitch characteristics → expected contact rate
  • Ensemble of Intervals: Combine prediction intervals from multiple models for better calibration
  • Real-Time Updates: Re-train weekly as more second-half data accumulates

Model Assumptions

  • Contact rate is a stable skill that can be projected (true for most players, but some show trend changes)
  • First-half sample sufficient to estimate true talent (violated for <100 swing hitters)
  • Second-half opponents similar in quality to first-half (approximately true at aggregate level)
  • No major mechanical changes or injuries between halves (unchecked assumption)
  • Linear relationships sufficient (ensemble includes non-linear XGBoost to relax this)
